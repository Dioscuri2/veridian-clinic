# The GP-Led Guide to Longevity Biomarkers
## 7 Missing Markers That Can Change the Way You Read Your Health

**By Veridian Clinic**  
**CQC-Registered | GP-Led | Preventive & Longevity Medicine**

Most people are told their blood tests are "normal" and assume that means risk is low.

The problem is that routine screening often focuses on late-stage disease markers rather than the earlier signals that shape long-term metabolic and cardiovascular health. In practice, the most useful longevity biomarkers are often the ones that are never checked, never explained properly, or hidden behind reassuring but incomplete language.

At Veridian, we look for the *missing links* — the markers that can reveal metabolic strain, vascular risk, inflammation, nutrient insufficiency, and early physiological drift long before disease is formally diagnosed.

This guide covers seven of the most clinically useful markers we use when assessing long-term health trajectory.

---

## Why these markers matter

A good longevity screen should not just ask whether a value falls inside a wide laboratory reference range. It should ask:

- What is moving in the wrong direction before disease appears?
- Which markers explain fatigue, weight resistance, brain fog, poor recovery, or rising cardiovascular risk?
- Which biomarkers change management early enough to alter trajectory?

These seven markers help answer those questions.

---

## 1. Fasting Insulin — the marker that often matters before glucose

### Why it matters
Fasting glucose can remain "normal" for years while insulin resistance is already developing in the background. That means someone may be told their sugars are fine, even while their body is having to produce more and more insulin to keep glucose under control.

Fasting insulin is often a much earlier signal of metabolic dysfunction than fasting glucose alone.

### What it can reveal
Raised fasting insulin may point to:
- early insulin resistance
- compensatory hyperinsulinaemia
- increased future risk of type 2 diabetes
- higher likelihood of abdominal fat gain, cravings, and energy crashes
- a metabolic environment associated with vascular disease and fatty liver

### Why glucose alone can mislead
Glucose is a lagging marker. By the time fasting glucose rises clearly above optimal levels, metabolic dysfunction may already have been progressing for years.

### Clinical interpretation
A patient with normal glucose but elevated fasting insulin is not "fine" in a preventive-medicine context. They may be in the early phase where targeted intervention is most effective.

### Takeaway
**If you want to spot metabolic strain early, fasting insulin is often more informative than glucose alone.**

## 2. ApoB — more useful than LDL for particle-related cardiovascular risk

### Why it matters
LDL cholesterol is widely used, but it does not always tell the full story. ApoB reflects the number of atherogenic lipoprotein particles capable of entering the arterial wall.

From a cardiovascular-risk perspective, particle number often matters more than cholesterol content alone.

### What it can reveal
Elevated ApoB may indicate:
- higher burden of artery-penetrating lipoproteins
- underestimated cardiovascular risk despite acceptable-looking LDL
- discordance between standard cholesterol measures and true particle burden
- increased long-term atherosclerotic risk

### Why LDL alone can mislead
Two people can have the same LDL cholesterol but very different particle numbers. ApoB helps clarify who is carrying greater atherosclerotic risk.

### Clinical interpretation
ApoB is particularly valuable in patients with metabolic syndrome, insulin resistance, raised triglycerides, or a family history of cardiovascular disease.

### Takeaway
**If LDL is the headline number, ApoB is often the deeper truth.**

## 3. Homocysteine — the missing link in vascular and methylation health

### Why it matters
Homocysteine is one of the most commonly overlooked markers in preventive care. When elevated, it can reflect impaired methylation, suboptimal B-vitamin status, or increased vascular stress.

It is not a standalone diagnosis, but it can be a very useful clue.

### What it can reveal
Raised homocysteine may be associated with:
- increased vascular risk
- impaired methylation pathways
- suboptimal folate, B12, or B6 status
- neurological vulnerability
- a mismatch between “normal” routine bloods and deeper biochemical imbalance

### Why it gets missed
It is not part of standard screening in many pathways, so patients can live with elevated homocysteine for years without explanation.

### Clinical interpretation
Homocysteine is best interpreted in context alongside B12, folate, clinical symptoms, and broader cardiovascular/metabolic risk.

### Takeaway
**Homocysteine can be the biochemical clue that links vascular risk, fatigue, and nutrient insufficiency.**

## 4. hs-CRP — a window into chronic low-grade inflammation

### Why it matters
High-sensitivity C-reactive protein (hs-CRP) is used to detect low-grade systemic inflammation. It does not tell you *where* inflammation is coming from, but it can tell you whether chronic inflammatory load may be contributing to risk.

Inflammation is one of the core processes that links metabolic disease, vascular disease, and accelerated ageing.

### What it can reveal
Raised hs-CRP may suggest:
- chronic inflammatory burden
- elevated cardiometabolic risk
- inflammatory contribution to fatigue and poor recovery
- an internal environment that may accelerate vascular damage over time

### Important caution
hs-CRP can also rise with infection, injury, or acute illness, so interpretation must consider timing and symptoms.

### Clinical interpretation
In the right context, persistently elevated hs-CRP may support a broader picture of metabolic strain, poor diet quality, excess visceral fat, inadequate sleep, or unresolved inflammatory drivers.

### Takeaway
**hs-CRP is not specific — but it is often revealing. Low-grade inflammation matters in longevity medicine.**

## 5. ALT and AST — early signals of metabolic liver stress

### Why they matter
ALT and AST are liver enzymes often checked in routine blood tests. They are usually used to screen for overt liver problems, but in preventive care they can also act as early signals of metabolic liver strain, especially when interpreted alongside insulin resistance, triglycerides, waist circumference, and energy symptoms.

### What they can reveal
Abnormal or high-normal trends may suggest:
- metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease (fatty liver)
- insulin resistance-related liver stress
- early hepatic strain before major symptoms appear
- mismatch between outward function and internal metabolic load

### Why this matters for longevity
Fatty liver is not just a liver issue. It is often a whole-body metabolic signal associated with insulin resistance, cardiovascular risk, and systemic dysfunction.

### Clinical interpretation
ALT and AST should not be read in isolation. Trend, ratio, and metabolic context matter more than a single number taken alone.

### Takeaway
**Liver enzymes can be an early warning sign that metabolism is under pressure, not just that the liver is “abnormal.”**

## 6. Ferritin — more than an iron test

### Why it matters
Ferritin is usually thought of as an iron-storage marker, but it can also behave as an inflammatory marker. Low ferritin may point to depleted iron stores; high ferritin can sometimes reflect inflammation, liver stress, metabolic dysfunction, or iron overload depending on the wider picture.

### What it can reveal
Ferritin can help flag:
- low iron stores contributing to fatigue, reduced performance, or hair loss
- inflammatory burden when ferritin is raised
- possible liver-metabolic stress when paired with other abnormal markers
- the need to differentiate between deficiency and inflammatory sequestration

### Why nuance matters
A ferritin result cannot be interpreted properly without context. A “normal” ferritin may still be suboptimal for some symptomatic patients, while a raised ferritin is not automatically iron overload.

### Clinical interpretation
Ferritin works best alongside full blood count, transferrin saturation, inflammatory markers, symptoms, and metabolic context.

### Takeaway
**Ferritin sits at the intersection of iron biology, inflammation, and metabolic interpretation.**

---

## 7. Vitamin D and B12 — foundational optimisation markers

### Why they matter
Not every longevity marker has to be exotic. Some of the most useful optimisation markers are foundational.

Vitamin D and B12 influence multiple systems relevant to long-term health, including energy, immune function, neurological function, musculoskeletal resilience, and recovery.

### What they can reveal
Suboptimal Vitamin D may contribute to:
- impaired immune resilience
- reduced musculoskeletal health
- poorer recovery and general vitality

Suboptimal B12 may contribute to:
- fatigue
- impaired concentration or neurological symptoms
- elevated homocysteine in some patients
- functional underperformance despite otherwise “acceptable” routine labs

### Why they still matter in advanced preventive care
Longevity medicine is not only about advanced biomarkers. It is also about making sure the fundamentals are not quietly undermining performance and resilience.

### Clinical interpretation
Values should be interpreted in context, not simply labelled normal or abnormal. Symptoms, diet pattern, gut health, medications, and broader biochemical patterns all matter.

### Takeaway
**Optimisation starts with foundations. Vitamin D and B12 are simple markers with outsized practical value.**

## How to think about these markers together

Each biomarker tells a different part of the story:

- **Fasting insulin** helps reveal early metabolic dysfunction
- **ApoB** sharpens cardiovascular risk assessment
- **Homocysteine** adds insight into methylation and vascular stress
- **hs-CRP** reflects inflammatory burden
- **ALT/AST** can reveal metabolic liver strain
- **Ferritin** helps distinguish iron issues from inflammatory or metabolic patterns
- **Vitamin D/B12** anchor foundational optimisation

On their own, each marker is useful.

Together, they form a much stronger picture of biological trajectory than a standard “everything looks fine” screen.

---

## Who should consider testing these markers?

These markers are often especially relevant if you:

- have a family history of heart disease, stroke, diabetes, or dementia
- feel tired despite being told blood tests are normal
- struggle with weight resistance or central weight gain
- notice cravings, energy crashes, or poor recovery
- want a more advanced view of cardiometabolic risk
- are interested in proactive, GP-led longevity optimisation
